Haptic Simulation Device

This device straps to the feet of patients and applies vibration to replicate the sensations of walking while receiving robotic therapy. I am currently leading its design under UCI's Biorobotics Lab for the Airstep Project, one that is developing a device that rolls up to the bed of patients with spinal cord injuries and replicates the motion of walking while laying down using a system of cables and pulleys. By referencing the patient's position in their gait cycle through data retrieved from the Airstep robot, this device provides haptic stimulation in a waveform pattern from heel to toe that mimics the sensations that would be felt during a regular step.

With clinical application in mind, the initial parameters for this design were that it had to supply a sufficient amount stimulation to the feet, and that it must be able to fit nearly all foot sizes and proportions. My team put a great deal of research into the primary mechanics that would go into the device, and we ultimately used this 3D printed three-segmented slotted rail design to allow for a wide range of size variation, fitting sizes all the way from a US 4 to a US 15.

One particular challenge that we encountered was that with this feature of varied size, we were unable to use traditional static length wiring methods. Being inspired by landline telephones I saw while I was out thrifting, I came up with the idea to use coiled telephone cables and RJ9 connectors to allow for a wide range of motion, along with the added benefit of making the device entirely modular. This feature lended itself excellently to our later stages of R&D where we began to test different types of motors, allowing us to quickly and painlessly swap components.

This past Halloween, the Airstep team had the amazing opportunity to bring our robot to Ranchos Los Amigos National Rehabilitation Hospital to test our most recent developments, the Haptic Simuation Device being one of them. Plenty of valuable feedback and information was retrieved during this test, an immensly notable one being the confirmation that the foot device does indeed work, a patient claiming that it increased the sensation in her entire lower body by an order of a magnitude. With this information in mind, we are continuing the development of the foot device, the above video showing a rough mockup of an ankle motion component that would help increase the realism of the gait process.
